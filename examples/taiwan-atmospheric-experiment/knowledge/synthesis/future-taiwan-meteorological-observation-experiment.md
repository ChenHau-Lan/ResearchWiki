# Future Taiwan Meteorological Observation Experiment

## Query

What should Taiwan do in a future meteorological observation experiment,
given the lineage from TAMEX to SoWMEX/TiMREX and TAHOPE/PRECIP?

## Short Answer

Design the next campaign as an observation-to-prediction system, not only as a
field measurement campaign. The evidence in this example suggests a
generational arc: TAMEX established terrain-rainfall process diagnosis,
SoWMEX/TiMREX added monsoon convection, radar microphysics, and model
verification, while TAHOPE/PRECIP moves toward storm microphysics, intensive
radar networks, and data assimilation for heavy-rainfall prediction.

The strongest future design is multi-platform, regime-aware, reading-maturity
tracked, and data-management-first.

## Evidence-Backed Recommendations

1. Treat TAMEX, SoWMEX/TiMREX, and TAHOPE/PRECIP as a design ladder.
   TAMEX-style terrain and Mei-yu process diagnosis should remain the base,
   but future campaigns should also include SoWMEX/TiMREX-style radar
   microphysics and TAHOPE/PRECIP-style prediction and assimilation products.

2. Co-design observation and forecast evaluation. SoWMEX IOP8 shows that
   dual-polarimetric radar observations can evaluate microphysics schemes and
   radar observation operators. A new campaign should define model-evaluation,
   nowcasting, and data-assimilation products before deployment.

3. Preserve dense multi-platform observations. Minimum families should include
   dual-polarimetric radar, rain gauges, disdrometers, soundings, wind
   profilers, dropsondes, aircraft or UAV transects, satellite/lidar context,
   aerosol chemistry, CCN spectra, and in-cloud microphysics where feasible.

4. Design by weather regime, not only by location. Sampling should distinguish
   Mei-yu fronts, southwest monsoon rainbands, typhoon rainfall, afternoon
   thunderstorms over Taipei Basin or mountain regions, windward lifting,
   lee-side response, coastal local circulation, and weak-synoptic summer
   convection.

5. Keep storm microphysics visible. TAHOPE/PRECIP IOP2 evidence shows that
   merged cells, ZDR columns, graupel growth, and terrain-induced circulation
   can be decisive for intense rainfall. Scan strategies should preserve
   vertical structure, cell-merger timing, and polarimetric signatures.

6. Pair heavy-rainfall dynamics with aerosol-cloud observations. Taiwan's
   terrain and monsoon environment make rainfall sensitive to flow, moisture,
   aerosol loading, cloud microphysics, and local circulation. Aerosol-cloud
   pages remain extension evidence, but they should influence the instrument
   list for a next-generation campaign.

7. Make data management an experiment objective. Dataset access, metadata,
   instrument calibration notes, quality flags, case catalogs, event IDs, and
   public-safe documentation should be planned before the field phase.

## Evidence Gaps

- Kuo and Chen 1990 TAMEX overview can start as a metadata draft, then should
  request user PDF/full text before any stable claims are promoted.
- Chang, Lee, and Liou 2015 SoWMEX/TiMREX microphysics paper is a high-value
  queue item but still needs user PDF/full text and locators.
- Yang et al. 2024 TAHOPE/PRECIP IOP3 data-assimilation paper should remain in
  the active paper queue until full text and locators are available.
- Operations plans, calibration documents, deployment maps, and data-policy
  documents are needed before making detailed instrument-placement decisions.
- The aerosol-cloud part currently relies on Cape Fuguei, Lulin, and modeling
  support; add more in-situ cloud-event papers across seasons and locations.
